Training like the pros, on amateur legs.

I'm 38, I live in Oslo, and I'm training by the Norwegian threshold method — the approach behind many of the world's best distance runners — to see how far a dedicated amateur can really get. The targets: 5K sub-17, 10K sub-35, half sub-1:20. Every run and every gym session goes here, in the open.

New here? The whole idea, in 20 seconds.

Most people think you get faster by running as hard as you can. This method does almost the opposite. You run at a pace that's comfortably hard — quick, but controlled enough that you're never wrecked — and you do it often. That effort level is your threshold. Stack up enough of it, week after week, and you get faster without breaking down. That's the whole trick. Everything below is me testing whether it actually works.

The goals
Sub-17, sub-35, sub-1:20.

Three barriers at roughly the same level of fitness — 5K under 17:00, 10K under 35:00, and the half under 1:20:00 (about 3:24 to 3:48 per km). They're a real jump from my current bests, so it's a long-term climb: build back past my PBs first, then break the barriers one at a time.

The Sunday long runs are what would turn that speed into a faster half.

Why train this way

The method comes from Marius Bakken, a former Norwegian 5000 m runner, and it now sits behind a lot of the sport's best. The core bet is simple: consistent, controlled quality beats occasional heroics. You improve by training hard enough, often enough, for long enough — not by emptying the tank every session.

In practice, the hard runs are chopped into intervals so I can hold threshold pace longer without tipping over, the easy days stay genuinely easy, and strength and hills build durability. I steer by feel and heart rate here; the purists add a finger-prick lactate meter for extra precision — a step I may take later.
